How to Help Someone With Depression: A Practical Guide

When someone you love is depressed, you want to help — but it's hard to know how. Here's what actually works, what to avoid, and how to take care of yourself in the process.

Medically reviewed by Dr. Agustin Arrieta, MD
Last updated: February 2026 · 11 min read

Watching someone you care about struggle with depression is painful. You may feel helpless, frustrated, or unsure of what to say. You might worry about making things worse.

Here's the truth: you don't need to be a therapist. You don't need perfect words. Your presence and consistency matter more than anything you say. This guide gives you practical, evidence-based strategies for being a meaningful source of support.

Communication

What to Say — and What Not to Say

The words you choose matter. Not because there's a perfect script, but because depression warps how people interpret language. Something you mean as encouraging can land as dismissive.

✓ Say This

  • "I'm here for you."
  • "You don't have to go through this alone."
  • "What would be most helpful right now?"
  • "I may not understand exactly how you feel, but I care about you."
  • "It's okay to not be okay."
  • "This isn't your fault."
  • "I'm not going anywhere."
  • "Would it help if I [specific action]?"

✗ Avoid This

  • "Just snap out of it."
  • "You have so much to be grateful for."
  • "It's all in your head."
  • "Have you tried just exercising more?"
  • "Other people have it worse."
  • "You don't look depressed."
  • "I know how you feel." (unless you truly do)
  • "You just need to stay positive."

Why these matter: Statements like "snap out of it" or "think positive" imply that depression is a choice — something the person could overcome with enough willpower. Depression is a medical condition. Telling someone to just "get over" their depression is like telling someone with diabetes to just will their blood sugar down.

Practical Support

Actions That Actually Help

Words matter, but actions often matter more. Depression drains energy for even basic tasks. Practical support can be life-changing.

1. Be Specific With Offers

"Let me know if you need anything" is well-intentioned but puts the burden on the depressed person to identify and ask for help — something depression makes extremely difficult. Instead, offer specific help:

  • "I'm going to the grocery store — can I pick up a few things for you?"
  • "I'll come by Saturday at 2pm. We can go for a walk or just hang out."
  • "I made extra dinner — I'm dropping some off tonight."
  • "I found a therapist who's taking new patients. Want me to call and make the appointment?"

2. Stay Present Without Pressuring

Keep showing up, even when they cancel plans or don't respond to texts. Depression causes withdrawal — it's a symptom of the illness, not a rejection of you. Consistency communicates safety.

  • Text without expecting replies: "Thinking of you" or "No need to respond — just wanted you to know I'm here."
  • Invite them to low-pressure activities. "Want to come sit on the porch with me?" is better than "Let's go to a party."
  • Be comfortable with silence. You don't need to fill every moment with conversation.

3. Help With Daily Tasks

Depression makes ordinary tasks feel monumental. Things that seem small to you can be enormous for someone in a depressive episode:

  • Help with dishes, laundry, or tidying up
  • Offer to drive them to appointments
  • Help with meals — even bringing takeout counts
  • Assist with bills or administrative tasks if they're falling behind
  • Walk the dog, pick up prescriptions, handle an errand

4. Encourage Professional Help (Gently)

Many people with depression resist treatment — out of stigma, hopelessness ("nothing will help"), or sheer lack of energy. You can encourage without pressuring:

  • Normalize therapy: "Lots of people see therapists — it's like going to a doctor for your mental health."
  • Offer to help with logistics: "I can help you find someone, or I'll drive you to the first appointment."
  • Share facts: "Depression is really treatable — 80-90% of people get better with the right treatment."
  • If they resist, don't push. Let them know the offer stands.

5. Learn About Depression

Understanding depression helps you depersonalize their behavior. When you know that irritability, withdrawal, cancelled plans, and lack of motivation are symptoms — not choices — you can respond with empathy instead of frustration.

Crisis Recognition

Recognizing a Mental Health Crisis

Knowing the warning signs of a crisis can save a life. Take any mention of suicide or self-harm seriously — even if it sounds casual.

Warning signs that require immediate action:

• Talking about wanting to die or kill themselves

• Looking for ways to end their life (searching online, acquiring means)

• Expressing feelings of hopelessness or having no reason to live

• Talking about being a burden to others

• Increasing alcohol or drug use

• Extreme mood swings — especially sudden calm after a period of depression

• Withdrawing from everything and everyone

• Giving away prized possessions

• Saying goodbye as if they won't see you again

What to Do in a Crisis

  1. Don't leave them alone if you believe they're in immediate danger.
  2. Ask directly: "Are you thinking about suicide?" — asking does NOT plant the idea. Research consistently shows that asking about suicide does not increase risk and can actually reduce distress.
  3. Call 988 (Suicide & Crisis Lifeline) — available 24/7 by call or text.
  4. Text HOME to 741741 (Crisis Text Line) if they prefer text.
  5. Go to the nearest emergency room if there's immediate danger.
  6. Remove access to lethal means if possible — firearms, medications, etc.

Myth: "Asking about suicide will put the idea in their head." Fact: Multiple studies confirm that asking about suicide does not increase suicidal ideation. It gives the person permission to talk about what they're experiencing and can be the first step toward getting help.

Caregiver Wellness

Taking Care of Yourself

Supporting someone with depression is emotionally demanding. Caregiver burnout is real, and it helps no one if you run yourself into the ground.

Set Boundaries

You can be supportive without being available 24/7. It's okay to say, "I love you and I want to help, but I need to take care of myself too." Boundaries aren't selfish — they're how you sustain your ability to help long-term.

Maintain Your Own Life

  • Keep up with your own friendships and hobbies
  • Don't cancel all your plans to be available
  • Exercise, eat well, and prioritize your own sleep
  • Talk to someone about what you're going through — a friend, family member, or your own therapist

Accept What You Can't Control

You cannot fix someone's depression. You can support, encourage, and love them — but their recovery is ultimately their journey. Let go of the idea that you should be able to make it better. That's not a failure on your part.

Seek Your Own Support

  • NAMI Family-to-Family: Free 8-session program for families of people with mental illness. nami.org
  • NAMI Support Groups: Peer-led support groups for caregivers, available nationwide and online.
  • Your own therapist: Having a space to process your own feelings is not a luxury — it's maintenance.

Supporting Different Relationships

Supporting a Partner/Spouse

Depression in a romantic relationship is especially challenging because it directly affects intimacy, communication, and shared responsibilities. Remember that your partner's withdrawal isn't about you. Couples therapy can be helpful — it gives both of you tools and a shared language.

Supporting a Parent

It can feel like role reversal when a parent becomes the one who needs care. Set limits on what you can provide, coordinate with siblings or other family, and remember that professional help is not a failure of family love.

Supporting a Friend

You might feel like you're "not doing enough" compared to family. But consistent friendship — the text, the check-in, the lunch invite — can be a lifeline for someone whose depression is isolating them from everyone else.

Supporting a Child or Teen

Adolescent depression requires professional evaluation. Watch for changes in academic performance, social withdrawal, irritability, substance use, and self-harm. Don't dismiss moodiness as "just being a teenager." Trust your instincts — if something feels wrong, it's worth getting assessed.

If you're in crisis

If you or someone you know is experiencing thoughts of self-harm or suicide, help is available 24/7:

988 Suicide & Crisis Lifeline: Call or text 988

Crisis Text Line: Text HOME to 741741

Frequently Asked Questions

Common Questions About Supporting Someone With Depression

What should you say to someone with depression?

Simple, validating statements work best: "I'm here for you," "You don't have to go through this alone," "What would be most helpful right now?" and "I care about you." Avoid trying to fix their feelings — just acknowledge them.

What should you not say to someone with depression?

Avoid: "Just snap out of it," "You have so much to be grateful for," "It's all in your head," "Have you tried exercising?," and "Other people have it worse." These minimize the condition and imply it's a choice. Depression is a medical condition, not a mood.

How do you help a depressed person who doesn't want help?

Stay present without pressuring. Let them know you care and that help is available when they're ready. Keep inviting them to things. Offer to handle logistics (making appointments, driving). Sometimes just being a consistent, non-judgmental presence is the most powerful support.

How do you know if someone with depression is in crisis?

Warning signs include: talking about dying, searching for means, hopelessness, feeling like a burden, increased substance use, extreme mood swings, giving away possessions, and saying goodbye. If you see these signs, call 988 or go to the nearest ER.

Can you cause someone's depression?

No. Depression is caused by genetics, brain chemistry, life events, and medical factors. While relationship stress can contribute, you are not the cause. Focus on being supportive rather than assigning blame.

How do you take care of yourself when supporting someone with depression?

Set boundaries, maintain your own social life, seek your own support (therapy, support groups like NAMI), accept that you can't fix their depression, and take breaks when you need them. Caregiver burnout helps no one.
